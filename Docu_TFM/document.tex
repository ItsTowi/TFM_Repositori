\chapter{Introduction}
In recent years, there has been a boom in 
artificial intelligence that is commercially
available and accessible to the general public. 
When these large language models first emerged, 
the industry realized that the models were black boxes, 
and if they wanted the model to learn something new, 
they had to retrain it (an expensive process).

\bigbreak
\noindent This caused three critical problems in LLMs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item  Hallucinations: The models confidently invent facts
    \item  Knowledge gap: They cannot know anything that happened after their training
    \item Lack of privacy: They cannot access your private or corporate documents.
\end{itemize}

In September 2020, researchers at Facebook AI 
Research published the first paper on 
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). 
RAG was developed to address these issues; 
before the model responds, it searches a database 
for information related to the question asked and 
provides context for it.

\bigbreak
These RAGs operate using semantic vector search: 
text is broken down into chunks, converted into 
numerical values, and stored. When you ask an LLM a 
question, the system searches for the chunks that are 
numerically most similar to your query, thereby 
providing the LLM with more context. This approach had a 
limitation: the LLM struggled to answer general questions 
based on the documents when using the RAG.

\bigbreak
To address this issue, GraphRAG was developed—combining 
LLMs with knowledge graphs, which contain interconnected
entities such as people, places, concepts, and 
relationships like “X works at Y.” This allows us 
to extract broader contextual insights from the data 
at our disposal.

\section{Problem Statement}

In this thesis, we aim to address the practical 
challenges involved in generating and using 
high-quality RAGs or GraphsRAGs. 
When using them, we identify three main issues: 
indexing time, indexing cost, and determining 
which systems are best suited to our needs—that is, 
the trade-offs involved in choosing one type of 
RAG over another.
